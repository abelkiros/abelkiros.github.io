02 · What drives me

Just do the thing.

I'm highly motivated and constantly striving for self-improvement in my health, my skills, my relationships, and my craft. I take pride in my work and I'm obsessive about learning. I believe we should be learning every single day. It's a privilege, and to me the moment we stop learning is the moment we kind of stop living. Planning matters, but in IT and security I've learned that the most valuable thing is often to just take action and do the thing. That approach builds confidence, exposes what you actually don't know yet, and helps you contribute real value instead of waiting to feel ready.

That's why I work on labs and personal projects outside of work. It's how I refine my craft and sharpen my skills. I approach it the same way I approach training: a little better each day, stacked consistently over time. I have a strong interest in cybersecurity, cloud infrastructure, and the intersection where both meet enterprise systems.

One thing I've been focused on lately is integrating AI and prompt engineering into my workflow. Not as a replacement for thinking, but as a force multiplier. Knowing how to actually use these tools well, while understanding where they fall short, is becoming one of the most valuable skills in tech. I'd rather be ahead of that curve than behind it.

03 · The journey

Heart of a Lion

አንበሳ

I've lost 100 pounds. Twice. The second time was harder.

The first journey started in 2013, when I traveled to Ethiopia and learned about my roots. Seeing where my family came from, and understanding what they sacrificed so my siblings and I could have the life we have, changed my perspective on the world. That trip wasn't just about food or exercise. It was about identity. አንበሳ (anbessa), meaning heart of a lion, became the word I came back with. It's the idea that no matter what life throws at you, you stay focused, you do the work, and you let the results speak for themselves.

The second journey started during COVID. I had gained all the weight back, and this time it was harder, because I already knew what being healthy felt like, and I had lost it. I started having panic attacks. I was afraid of developing real health conditions. Climbing back when you already know how high the top is, and you know exactly how much work it takes to get there, requires a different kind of discipline than doing it the first time.

What got me through the second climb was three things: willpower, faith, and the people around me. I couldn't have done it without my family and friends. They believed in the version of me I was trying to become before I did. Faith, trusting God, was the steady part underneath the effort, especially on the days the effort wasn't enough by itself.

Today I'm stronger than I've ever been. I'm benching 225 for 8, doing weighted pull-ups and dips, and holding a standard of health I used to think wasn't for people like me. But the part that matters isn't the numbers. It's what I learned from being on both sides of that climb. I know what it costs to rebuild yourself. I know how much difference the right support makes. And that's why I feel I owe it to be in a position to help the people coming up behind me, whether that's family, friends, or the users I support every night at work. It's also part of why I'm building a career in cybersecurity. It's a field that exists to protect people, and after being on the receiving end of that kind of support, being on the giving end feels like the right use of my time.

Discipline under pressure is discipline under pressure. Whether it's a CrowdStrike outage at 3am or rebuilding yourself back to who you know you can be, the work is the work, and you do it one day at a time.
